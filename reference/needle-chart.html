Needle Chart

Using the wrong needle is one of the most common causes of skipped stitches, fabric damage, and thread breakage. This chart tells you which needle to use for every fabric type.

Tip: Print this chart and keep it near your machine.

⚠️ Always use the right needle

Using a universal needle on knit fabric will create runs and holes. Using a fine needle on denim will break it immediately. The right needle makes sewing easier and protects your fabric.

💡 When in doubt

Start with a Universal needle size 80/12. It works on most woven fabrics and is the best all-purpose starting point.

Needle Type Size Range Best For Tip
Universal 70/10 – 110/18 Most woven fabrics: cotton, linen, synthetic wovens, blends Best starting needle. Size 80/12 works on most medium-weight wovens.
Ballpoint / Stretch 70/10 – 110/18 Knit fabrics: jersey, t-shirt fabric, ribbing, fleece Rounded tip pushes fabric threads aside instead of piercing — prevents runs in knits.
Jeans / Denim 90/14 – 110/18 Denim, canvas, heavy woven fabrics, multiple layers Strong shaft and sharp point for thick, tightly woven fabrics. Use size 100/16 or 110/18 for heavy denim.
Sharp / Microtex 60/8 – 90/14 Silk, fine cotton, microfiber, tightly woven fabrics Very sharp, thin point for precise stitch placement on delicate fabrics.
Embroidery 75/11 – 90/14 Machine embroidery, decorative stitching, metallic thread Extra-large eye prevents metallic and specialty threads from fraying.
Quilting 75/11 – 90/14 Quilting through multiple fabric layers and batting Tapered point for piecing and quilting. Reduces skipped stitches at high speed.
Twin Needle 1.6mm – 4.0mm (gap between needles) Decorative hems on knit garments, pintucks, parallel topstitching Requires two thread spools. Creates two parallel rows on top, zigzag on the underside. Hem stretches with the fabric.

Understanding Needle Sizes

Needle sizes are written as two numbers separated by a slash, for example 80/12. The first number is the European sizing system and the second is the American sizing system — they refer to the same needle. A larger number means a thicker, stronger needle. Use thinner needles for delicate fabrics and thicker needles for heavy fabrics.

As a general rule: the lighter and more delicate the fabric, the lower the number you need. Fine silk calls for a 60/8 or 65/9; heavy upholstery canvas may need a 110/18 or even 120/19.

When to Replace Your Needle

Replace your needle every 6–8 hours of actual sewing time. Needles dull faster than most people expect, and a dull or damaged needle is one of the most common causes of poor stitch quality.

Signs you need a new needle:

  • A popping or thudding sound when the needle enters the fabric
  • Skipped stitches that weren't happening before
  • Puckered or distorted fabric along the seam
  • Frequent thread breakage
  • The needle feels "sticky" or catches when entering fabric

Needles are inexpensive — a pack of 10 costs less than a dollar. When in doubt, replace it. A fresh needle solves a surprising number of mysterious sewing problems.
