Machine Basics

The sewing machine is not as intimidating as it looks. Once you thread it successfully once, it becomes second nature. This lesson covers everything you need to know before sewing your first seam.

Before You Start — A Note on Fear

Many beginners are genuinely afraid of the sewing machine. This is completely normal. The machine has a needle that moves very fast, it makes noise, and the foot pedal feels unpredictable the first time you press it.

Here is what you need to know: you control the speed entirely. The foot pedal is like a car's accelerator — press gently and it goes slowly. The machine only goes fast when you push the pedal hard. In the beginning, you'll barely press the pedal at all, and that's exactly right.

You cannot break the machine by going slowly. You cannot break it by stopping mid-seam. You cannot break it by taking your foot off the pedal completely. The worst thing that happens when you go slowly is that the stitches are fine and the seam is a little wobbly — and that's entirely fixable with a seam ripper.

The goal for this lesson is not to sew anything finished. It's to thread the machine, understand how it works, and make a few lines of practice stitching on a scrap. Speed and confidence come with practice. Focus on understanding, not performance.

Threading the Top Thread — Step by Step

Every machine threads slightly differently, so keep your manual nearby and compare these steps to your specific machine's diagram. The sequence below describes the standard threading path found on most modern domestic sewing machines.

  1. Raise the needle to its highest position. Turn the handwheel (on the right side of the machine) toward you — always toward you, never away from you — until the needle is at its highest point. This ensures the needle is out of the way and the take-up lever is positioned correctly for threading.
  2. Raise the presser foot lever. The presser foot lever is at the back of the machine, just above and behind the needle. Lifting it does two things: raises the presser foot off the needle plate, and — critically — releases the tension discs inside the machine. The thread must pass between these tension discs when you thread, and they only open when the presser foot is up.
  3. Place your thread spool on the spool pin. The spool pin is the horizontal or vertical pin at the top of the machine. Most machines include a spool cap — a small disc that sits over the spool to keep it from wobbling or jumping off during sewing. Use it.
  4. Guide the thread through the first thread guide. This is usually a hook or a slot at the top of the machine, near the spool pin. Pull the thread across the top of the machine following the printed arrows or numbered guides on the machine body.
  5. Pull the thread DOWN through the tension disc channel. This is the narrow slot or channel on the front of the machine. You should feel or hear a slight resistance as the thread slips between the tension discs — that resistance is correct. If the thread slides through with no resistance at all, the presser foot may still be down. Check it.
  6. Guide the thread UP through the take-up lever. The take-up lever is the moving arm or hook near the top of the machine's front face. It moves up and down as you sew, pulling a fresh length of thread for each stitch. Thread goes through the hole or hook in the take-up lever.
  7. Guide the thread DOWN again through any lower thread guides. Most machines have one or two small hooks or guides between the take-up lever and the needle that direct the thread smoothly toward the needle.
  8. Thread the needle. On most home machines, thread goes from front to back (away from you). Some thread left to right. Your machine's manual will specify. Use good light — a needle threader is helpful here.
  9. Leave a 6-inch tail of thread hanging free. Don't pull the thread through the presser foot slot or hold it taut. Let it hang. You'll need that tail when you start sewing.

📌 Important — Always thread with the presser foot RAISED. If the presser foot is down, the tension discs are closed and the thread won't seat properly between them. This is the single most common threading mistake beginners make. Threading with the foot down causes loose stitches, thread looping on the underside of the fabric, and thread breakage — all of which are baffling if you don't know why they're happening. Foot up. Always.

Winding and Loading the Bobbin

The bobbin is the small spool that holds the lower thread. Upper thread and lower thread interlock with each other to form every stitch — without a correctly wound and loaded bobbin, the machine cannot stitch.

Winding the Bobbin

  1. Place an empty bobbin on the bobbin winder pin (usually on the top of the machine, to the right of the spool pin).
  2. Thread your spool onto the spool pin and guide the thread through the bobbin winding thread guide (separate from the main threading path — follow your machine's marked path for bobbin winding).
  3. Wrap the thread through one of the holes in the side of the bobbin a few times to anchor it, then hold the thread tail.
  4. Slide the bobbin pin to the right (or press it down, depending on the machine) to engage the winding mechanism. This usually disengages the needle so it doesn't move while winding.
  5. Press the foot pedal. The bobbin will spin and fill with thread. Most machines stop automatically when the bobbin is full. If yours doesn't, stop when the thread is level with the edges of the bobbin — don't overfill.
  6. Clip the thread, slide the bobbin pin back to its original position.

Loading the Bobbin

Most modern machines have a top-loading (drop-in) bobbin. Some older machines are front-loading with a removable bobbin case.

Top-loading (drop-in): Slide open the cover on the needle plate. Drop the bobbin in, following the printed arrow to ensure correct thread direction. Pull the thread through the slot in the bobbin holder and follow the threading path (usually a J-shape) as indicated by the arrows engraved on the machine. A correctly loaded bobbin should resist slightly when you pull the thread — like it's under gentle tension.

Front-loading (with bobbin case): Remove the bobbin case by pressing the release lever. Drop the bobbin into the case, pull the thread through the slot and under the tension spring. Click the bobbin case back into the machine with the finger (the protruding part) pointing up.

Raising the Bobbin Thread

Before you can sew, you need to bring the bobbin thread up through the needle plate so both threads are on top and ready. This step confuses a lot of beginners the first time, but it makes sense once you see it happen.

  1. Hold the upper thread loosely with your left hand, letting it drape over the needle plate.
  2. Turn the handwheel toward you one full rotation. The needle will go down into the bobbin area and come back up.
  3. As the needle comes back up, it will bring a loop of bobbin thread with it. The upper thread, which you were holding, will have looped around and caught the bobbin thread.
  4. Use a pin or your finger to hook the loop and pull it up through the needle plate opening. You now have both threads — a loop of bobbin thread and the upper thread tail.
  5. Pull both threads to the back or side of the machine, leaving about 6-inch tails. These tails prevent thread tangles at the start of your first seam.

Understanding Tension

Thread tension is one of the most misunderstood settings on the machine. When beginners see loopy thread on the back of their fabric or thread pulling tight on the top, they often assume something is broken. Almost always, it's a threading error or a tension setting that needs adjustment.

The tension dial (or digital setting) controls how tightly the machine grips the upper thread as it forms each stitch. The goal is to have the two threads meet and interlock in the exact middle of the fabric layers, so neither thread shows on the opposite side.

  • Correct tension: The top of the fabric shows clean, even upper thread. The bottom shows clean, even lower (bobbin) thread. You can't see any bobbin thread from the top, or any upper thread from the bottom.
  • Upper thread showing on the bottom: The upper tension is too tight (or the bobbin thread is too loose). The upper thread is being pulled too hard, dragging it through to the underside. Try reducing the tension number slightly.
  • Bobbin thread showing on the top: The upper tension is too loose. The upper thread isn't being gripped firmly enough and the bobbin thread is winning the tug-of-war. Try increasing the tension number slightly.

Most machines have a default tension setting of 4 or 5, and that works fine for standard cotton fabric with all-purpose thread. Always test your tension on a folded piece of the same fabric you'll use in your project — not a random scrap of something else — before you start sewing.

Stitch Length

Stitch length is measured in millimeters and refers to how much fabric passes under the needle between each stitch. Shorter stitches hold more tightly and are harder to remove. Longer stitches are looser, easier to remove, and useful for temporary stitching.

  • 2.0–2.5mm — Tight stitch for fine or delicate fabrics. Also used for curved seams that need to hold firmly.
  • 2.5–3.0mm — Standard stitch length for most sewing on medium-weight fabrics. This is where you'll spend most of your time.
  • 3.0–3.5mm — Slightly longer, suitable for heavier fabrics like denim or canvas.
  • 3.5–4.0mm — Machine basting. Long enough to be easy to remove with a seam ripper, but not so long that the layers shift during construction.
  • 4.0–5.0mm — Maximum basting length. Rarely used for anything permanent. Good for gathering (bunching fabric) by pulling the bobbin thread.

When in doubt, start with 2.5mm and adjust from there based on the fabric behavior.

Starting and Stopping

Every seam needs to be locked at both ends so the stitches don't unravel. You do this with backstitching — sewing backward for a few stitches over your starting and ending point.

  1. Place your fabric under the presser foot so the raw edge (where you'll start sewing) is about ½ inch from the needle.
  2. Lower the presser foot lever.
  3. Hold the thread tails to the back to prevent them from being pulled down into the machine and tangling.
  4. Press and hold the reverse button (or lever), then press the foot pedal gently. Sew backward 3–4 stitches.
  5. Release the reverse button and sew forward. Guide the fabric gently — don't pull or push, just guide. Let the feed dogs do the moving.
  6. At the end of the seam, stop with the needle down in the fabric (most machines have a setting for this, or you can turn the handwheel).
  7. Press and hold the reverse button and sew backward 3–4 stitches over the end of your seam.
  8. Stop sewing. Raise the needle to its highest position.
  9. Raise the presser foot lever.
  10. Pull the fabric toward the back-left of the machine — not straight back, not to the right. The back-left direction keeps the thread from unthreading the needle as it pulls free.
  11. Cut the threads, leaving 3–4 inch tails on both the fabric and the machine.

⚠️ Common Mistake — Threading with the presser foot down. Always raise the presser foot before threading. If you don't, the tension discs stay closed and the thread won't be gripped correctly. The result is loose stitches on the bottom of the fabric, thread looping and tangling, or thread breaking mid-seam. It's a frustrating problem with a simple fix: foot up before you thread, every single time.

⚠️ Common Mistake — Not leaving thread tails after cutting. If you cut threads flush with the fabric immediately after backstitching, the thread can still work itself loose — especially with slippery synthetic thread. Always leave 3–4 inch tails. For seams that will be enclosed (inside a pillow, inside a waistband), pull the tails to the inside before cutting. For exposed seams, trim to about ½ inch after the project is finished.

💡 Tip — Always test your tension and stitch settings on a scrap of the same fabric you're using for your project, not a random scrap from your bin. Tension, stitch length, and even needle size can all need adjustment depending on fabric weight, weave, and fiber content. Two minutes of testing on a scrap saves you the heartbreak of discovering your settings are wrong halfway through your project fabric.
