Reading Patterns

A sewing pattern is a set of instructions and paper templates that tell you exactly how to cut and assemble a garment or project. Learning to read a pattern opens up almost unlimited project possibilities.

Lesson 8 of 10

The Pattern Envelope — Front

A commercial sewing pattern comes in a printed envelope. Before you ever open the envelope, the front tells you a great deal about what you're buying.

  • Finished garment photo or illustration: Shows what the completed project looks like. Keep in mind that professional samples are usually sewn in carefully chosen fabric and photographed with expert styling — your result will depend heavily on your fabric choice.
  • Pattern number: The manufacturer's reference number for this specific pattern design.
  • Difficulty rating: Usually labeled as Beginner, Easy, Intermediate, Advanced, or something similar. Take these ratings seriously as a beginner — "Beginner" patterns have fewer pattern pieces, simpler construction, and less fitting involved.
  • Available views: Many patterns include multiple versions — View A, View B, etc. — with slight design variations (different neckline, length, or sleeve). Each view may require slightly different materials.
  • Available sizes: Printed on the front. Sizes are listed as pattern sizes, not clothing sizes.
  • Fabric suggestions: A general description of the fabric types the pattern was designed for — wovens vs. knits, lightweight vs. medium-weight, with or without nap.

The Pattern Envelope — Back

The back of the envelope is where the practical information lives. Read it carefully before buying.

  • Size chart: A table of body measurements (bust, waist, hip) corresponding to each pattern size. This is how you determine which size to cut.
  • Fabric yardage table: Shows how many yards of fabric you need for each size and each fabric width (usually 45 inches and 60 inches). Fabric width affects how many pattern pieces fit across it.
  • Notions list: Everything else you need — zipper (and its length), buttons (size and quantity), elastic (width and length), interfacing, seam binding, hooks and eyes. Read this list and have everything ready before you start.
  • Suggested fabric types: More specific than the front — may list actual fabric names like "cotton poplin, crepe, challis" and may specify "do not use plaids or large prints."
  • Finished garment measurements: The actual measurements of the completed garment at the bust, waist, and hip — including ease (the extra room built in for movement and comfort). This is different from your body measurements and shows you exactly how the finished item will fit.

Choosing Your Size — The Critical Step

This is the most important, and most misunderstood, step in working with sewing patterns.

Never choose your pattern size based on your clothing size. Clothing sizes are arbitrary numbers that vary significantly between brands and countries. A size 10 dress from one manufacturer is completely different from a size 10 dress from another. Pattern sizes are standardized and based directly on body measurements.

How to measure yourself

  1. Full bust: Measure around the fullest part of your chest, keeping the tape measure parallel to the floor and not too tight.
  2. Natural waist: Measure around the narrowest part of your torso, usually about an inch above your belly button. Bend sideways — your waist is where you crease.
  3. Full hip: Measure around the fullest part of your hips and seat, usually 7 to 9 inches below your natural waist.

Using the size chart

Compare your measurements to the size chart on the pattern envelope. Your bust, waist, and hip measurements may correspond to different sizes — this is normal. For a top or blouse, use the bust measurement. For pants or a skirt, use the hip measurement. For a dress, use the largest measurement and adjust.

If you fall between two sizes, cut the larger size. It is always easier to take a garment in (sew closer to the seam line, making it smaller) than to let it out (which requires seam allowance you may not have).

Inside the Pattern Envelope

Open the envelope carefully and you'll find two things:

Tissue paper pattern pieces
Thin, printed paper templates — usually pale yellow or white — for every piece of fabric in the garment. Each piece is numbered and labeled. Pattern pieces for multiple sizes are often printed on the same tissue, nested inside each other as a series of concentric cutting lines.
Instruction sheet
A large folded sheet with step-by-step assembly instructions, diagrams, a key to pattern symbols, fabric layout diagrams, and other guidance. Read this entire sheet before you begin cutting. Understanding the complete assembly order before you start prevents mistakes that require unsewing.

Before cutting your fabric, press the tissue pieces with a dry, cool iron to remove the fold creases. Crumpled tissue is difficult to pin accurately and can cause cutting errors.

Pattern Symbols

Pattern pieces are covered in symbols that carry specific meaning. Learning them is like learning to read a new language — once you know them, patterns become easy to follow.

Symbol What It Means
Grainline arrow (long arrow with heads at both ends) Place this piece on the fabric so this arrow runs parallel to the selvage (the finished edge of the fabric). Grain placement is critical — off-grain pieces hang crookedly and don't drape correctly.
Fold line (arrow pointing to a dotted line or bracket) Place this edge of the pattern piece directly on the fold of the fabric. Do not cut along the fold. The finished piece will be a mirror image — twice the width.
Cutting line (solid outer line, may be bold) Cut along this line. In multi-size patterns, each size has its own cutting line.
Seam line (dashed line inside the cutting line) The line your machine stitching should follow. The space between the cutting line and seam line is the seam allowance.
Notches (small triangles or diamonds on the cutting line) Cut these outward, into the seam allowance — never inward into the fabric. Notches are matching points that tell you which edge connects to which. Single notch matches to single notch, double notch matches to double notch.
Circles and squares Match these between two pieces being joined. They indicate specific alignment points — corners, pocket placement, zipper stops.
Dots Pivot points (where you turn a corner while sewing), gathering start/stop points, or critical matching points.
Lengthen/shorten line (two parallel horizontal lines) If you need to adjust the length of a pattern piece, cut and spread (to lengthen) or fold (to shorten) along this line. Adjusting here keeps the proportions correct.

Reading the Layout Diagram

The instruction sheet always includes one or more layout diagrams — a bird's-eye view showing how to fold your fabric and arrange the pattern pieces on it before cutting.

The layout diagram shows:

  • How to fold the fabric (lengthwise with right sides together, crosswise, single layer, etc.)
  • Which pattern pieces to place on the fold (shown shaded or with a specific symbol)
  • Which pieces to cut from a double layer (you get two mirror-image pieces at once)
  • Which pieces to cut from a single layer (usually when a piece is not symmetrical)
  • The grainline arrow direction for each piece, and where the selvage falls

There are usually multiple layout diagrams — one for each fabric width and sometimes for different views or sizes. Use the one that matches your fabric width and the view you're making.

Always follow the layout diagram. Pattern pieces placed off-grain cause finished garments to hang crookedly, twist to one side, or pull in unexpected directions. Even small grain errors are visible in the finished item.

Cutting Out Accurately

Accurate cutting is the foundation of accurate sewing. If pieces are cut wrong, no amount of careful stitching will fix the result.

  1. Position each pattern piece according to the layout diagram. Place pieces on fold lines where indicated. Align grainline arrows with the selvage.
  2. Verify grain alignment with a ruler. Measure from one end of the grainline arrow to the selvage, then from the other end. Both measurements must be identical. Adjust the piece until they match, then pin.
  3. Pin the pattern to the fabric at regular intervals — every 4 to 6 inches along curves, more frequently at corners. Pins should be within the seam allowance, parallel to the cutting line.
  4. Cut just outside the cutting line, letting the scissors graze the line rather than cutting it off entirely. Use long, smooth strokes with fabric shears. Do not use paper scissors — they're too blunt and will drag the fabric.
  5. Transfer all markings (notches, circles, squares, dots) to the fabric before removing the tissue paper. Use tailor's chalk, a fabric marker, or tailor's tacks (loops of thread). Notches can be cut outward; dots and circles can be marked with chalk through a pin hole in the tissue.

Remove tissue paper only after all markings are transferred. The tissue is the key to accurate assembly — once it's off the fabric, the marks on it are gone.

⚠️ Common Mistake — Choosing pattern size by clothing size. A person who wears size 10 clothing may need a size 14 pattern — because pattern sizes use actual body measurements, not vanity sizing. Clothing sizes in modern retail are significantly smaller than their labels suggest. Always measure your body, compare to the size chart on the pattern envelope, and cut the size that matches your measurements.

📌 Important — Mark all pattern symbols (notches, circles, squares) on your fabric before removing the tissue paper pattern pieces. These marks are how you know where to match pieces, sew buttonholes, attach pockets, and place other details. Once the tissue paper is off, you have no reference for where those marks were supposed to go.
