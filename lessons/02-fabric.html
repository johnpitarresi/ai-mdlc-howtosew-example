Getting Started

Fabric Fundamentals

The fabric you choose will determine how easy or hard your project is. Start with the right fabric and everything else gets easier.

Woven vs. Knit Fabric

The most important fabric distinction you'll ever learn is this one: fabric is either woven or knit, and the difference changes everything about how you work with it.

Woven Fabric

Woven fabric is made by interlacing two sets of threads at right angles to each other — like a grid. Picture the threads going horizontally (the weft) crossing over and under the threads going vertically (the warp). This structure gives woven fabric its key characteristics:

  • It does not stretch much in either the horizontal or vertical direction (though it has significant stretch on the diagonal, called the bias).
  • It frays when cut — the interlaced threads at a cut edge have nothing holding them in place and will begin to unravel. This is why seam finishing matters (covered in Lesson 6).
  • It holds its shape well and presses beautifully.

Examples of woven fabrics: cotton quilting fabric, linen, denim, canvas, poplin, chambray, chiffon, satin, twill.

Knit Fabric

Knit fabric is made from a single yarn (or set of yarns) that loops through itself continuously — like a hand-knit sweater, but done by machine at high speed. This loop structure gives knit fabric very different properties:

  • It stretches, especially horizontally. Many knits stretch in all directions.
  • It does not fray when cut — the loops simply don't unravel the way woven threads do. This means knits don't require the same kind of seam finishing.
  • It can be trickier to handle at the machine because it stretches as it feeds through.

Examples of knit fabrics: jersey (T-shirt fabric), interlock, fleece, ribbing, stretch velvet.

For beginners: start with wovens. Woven fabric stays where you put it, presses well, feeds evenly through your machine, and behaves predictably. Knits require slightly different techniques — a stretch stitch or a ballpoint needle — and are better tackled once you have a few projects under your belt.

Natural vs. Synthetic Fibers

Beyond structure (woven vs. knit), fabrics are categorized by the fiber they're made from. This affects how they feel, how they behave under the iron, and how easy they are to sew.

Natural Fibers

  • Cotton — The best beginner fiber, full stop. It's easy to cut, easy to pin, feeds smoothly through the machine, presses beautifully, and is widely available in every color and pattern imaginable. It breathes well, washes easily, and is forgiving of mistakes. Start here.
  • Linen — Strong, slightly stiff, and has a lovely natural texture. It wrinkles easily but presses out just as easily. A step up from cotton in terms of behavior — slightly more slippery and prone to fraying — but still very manageable for beginners.
  • Wool — Warm, forgiving, and beautiful for structured garments. Wool is actually quite beginner-friendly in terms of construction — it can be eased and shaped with steam. The challenge is cost and care (many wools are dry-clean only or require careful hand washing).
  • Silk — Beautiful, lustrous, and notoriously difficult. Silk is slippery, shifts while cutting, shifts while sewing, and shows every needle hole permanently. It also requires careful ironing (high heat damages it). Leave silk for when you have solid fundamentals.

Synthetic Fibers

  • Polyester — Wrinkle-resistant and durable, but slippery and prone to static. It doesn't press as crisply as natural fibers and can be damaged by high iron temperatures. Avoid as a beginner fabric.
  • Nylon — Very strong and often used for bags, activewear, and outerwear. Slippery and requires careful heat management when pressing.
  • Acrylic — A cheaper substitute for wool in some applications, but generally not pleasant to sew or wear. Not recommended for beginners or anyone else, frankly.

Blended Fibers

Many fabrics combine fibers to get the best of both. Cotton-polyester blends are excellent for beginners: they behave almost as well as 100% cotton in terms of sewability, but they're more wrinkle-resistant and often more affordable. A 60/40 or 65/35 cotton-poly blend is a solid choice for first projects.

Understanding Grain Lines

Grain refers to the direction of the threads in a woven fabric. Cutting and sewing "on grain" is fundamental to how finished items hang, drape, and behave. Grain problems are almost impossible to fix after the fact, so understanding grain now saves a lot of frustration later.

  • Lengthwise grain (warp) — The threads that run parallel to the selvage, down the length of the fabric as it comes off the bolt. This is the strongest direction because these threads are stretched taut during weaving. Garment patterns are almost always placed with the grain line parallel to the lengthwise grain.
  • Crosswise grain (weft) — The threads that run perpendicular to the selvage, across the width of the fabric. Slightly more stretch than the lengthwise grain. Some pattern pieces are deliberately cut on the crosswise grain to take advantage of this.
  • Bias — The true bias is exactly 45 degrees to both grain lines. Fabric cut on the bias has maximum stretch and drapes very differently than fabric cut on grain. Bias-cut garments are elegant and flowing but much more challenging to sew. Bias tape (used for binding edges) is cut on the bias specifically so it can stretch around curves.

Why does grain matter? If you cut a pattern piece off-grain — say, the grain line is tilted 10 degrees from the selvage instead of parallel — the finished garment piece will twist as it hangs, pull in unexpected directions, and generally look wrong in a way that's hard to name but immediately visible. A shirt sleeve cut off-grain will rotate forward or backward on the arm. A skirt cut off-grain will twist around the body. Always take the time to align your pattern pieces to the grain.

The Selvage

The selvage (also spelled selvedge) is the finished edge that runs down both long sides of the fabric as it comes off the loom. Unlike cut edges, the selvage doesn't fray — it's bound in the weaving process. You can often see it as a slightly different texture or color on the edge of the fabric, sometimes with the manufacturer's name, color information, or dots printed on it.

The selvage runs parallel to the lengthwise grain. This is extremely useful: if you're trying to find the lengthwise grain on a piece of fabric, you find the selvage. Pattern instructions will say things like "place grain line parallel to selvage" — this is how you ensure correct grain alignment.

In most projects, the selvage is trimmed off and discarded. Don't sew seams into the selvage — it often shrinks at a different rate than the rest of the fabric and can cause puckering.

Pre-Washing Your Fabric

Most natural-fiber fabrics — especially cotton and linen — shrink when they're washed for the first time. Sometimes a little, sometimes a lot (cotton can shrink 3–5% or more). If you sew your project first and wash it second, the finished item may pucker along seams, become too small, or distort as different sections shrink at different rates.

The solution is simple: always pre-wash your fabric before cutting and sewing.

How to pre-wash:

  1. Wash the fabric the same way you plan to wash the finished item — same temperature, same cycle, same detergent. If the finished pillow will go through warm machine wash, pre-wash the fabric in warm water. Don't pre-wash in cold if you'll eventually wash it in hot.
  2. Dry the fabric the same way — machine dry if that's what you'll do with the finished item, or air dry if you'll air dry it.
  3. Press the fabric before cutting. Pre-washed fabric wrinkles. A well-pressed piece cuts more accurately and sits flat while you work with it.

Synthetics generally don't shrink much and some sewers skip pre-washing for polyester. However, pre-washing also removes any manufacturing chemicals or sizing (stiffening agents) from the fabric, which makes it more pleasant to handle. It's a good habit regardless of fiber content.

Best Beginner Fabrics

When choosing fabric for your first few projects, look for these characteristics: medium weight (not too sheer, not too stiff), woven construction, and natural or natural-blend fiber content. Here are specific recommendations:

  • 100% cotton quilting fabric — This is the gold standard beginner fabric. It's sold in fat quarters and by the yard at every fabric and craft store. It's stiff enough to cut accurately, holds pins firmly, feeds smoothly through the machine, presses flat and crisp, and comes in literally thousands of prints and colors. If in doubt, use quilting cotton.
  • Cotton-polyester blend — Slightly softer and drapier than quilting cotton, widely available, and very forgiving. Excellent for small home goods and simple garments.
  • Canvas — A heavy, tightly woven fabric (usually cotton) that's ideal for bags and structured home accessories. It holds its shape without any interfacing and is easy to sew. Use a jeans needle (90/14) for heavier weights.

Fabrics to avoid for now:

  • Polyester satin or charmeuse — Slippery, shifts on the cutting table, shifts under the presser foot, and shows every pin hole.
  • Silk in any form — Beautiful, but requires skill and patience you'll develop over time.
  • Stretch knits — Need different handling, different needles, and often a different stitch. Save these for after you've mastered wovens.
  • Very sheer fabrics (chiffon, organza) — Hard to cut accurately, shift constantly, and require special techniques at the machine.

⚠️ Common Mistake — Not pre-washing. A finished pillow cover that shrinks or puckers in the first wash is a genuinely sad experience — especially after all the work you put in. Pre-washing takes 45 minutes and costs nothing. Always pre-wash.

💡 Tip — Buy an extra ¼ yard of fabric on every project. You'll use the extra for testing machine tension and stitch length, practicing seams before touching the real piece, and fixing mistakes. An extra quarter yard at $2–$4 is insurance that's always worth buying.
