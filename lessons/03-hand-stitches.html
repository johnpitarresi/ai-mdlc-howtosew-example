First Skills

Hand Stitches

Before you touch your machine, learn to sew by hand. Hand stitching builds your understanding of how thread holds fabric together — and it's essential for finishing and repairs.

Threading a Needle

Threading a needle sounds simple but there's a right way and a wrong way. Getting this right means fewer tangles and wasted thread.

  1. Cut your thread no longer than 18 inches. Longer thread is the enemy. It tangles on itself, forms knots mid-stitch, and generally creates frustration. 18 inches is plenty for most tasks. If you run out, simply cut a new length.
  2. Thread the cut end through the needle eye. The end of the thread that you cut is slightly crisper and more compact than the end still attached to the spool. Use the cut end — it's easier to thread. If you try to thread the spool end, the fibers spread out and resist going through the eye.
  3. Use a needle threader if needed. A needle threader is a small tool with a thin wire loop — you push the wire loop through the needle eye, put the thread through the wire loop, then pull the wire back through the eye, bringing the thread with it. No shame in using one; many professionals do.
  4. Pull about 6 inches through the eye. You'll work with the long end (the one still attached to the spool or the longer cut length). The short tail just needs to be long enough not to pull back through the eye while you work.

Starting Your Thread

You need to anchor your thread before you begin stitching, otherwise your first stitch will just pull right through the fabric. There are several methods; the waste knot is the cleanest for visible work.

The Waste Knot Method

  1. Make a knot at the end of your thread (loop it around your finger, roll it off, pull tight — or simply tie an overhand knot).
  2. On the right side (the visible side) of your fabric, push the needle down through the fabric about 1 inch away from where you want to start stitching, going from the right side to the wrong side. Pull through until the knot sits on top of the fabric on the right side.
  3. Bring your needle up at your actual starting point and begin stitching.
  4. After you've taken 4–5 stitches, clip the waste knot with your small scissors. The short tail of thread is now locked under your stitches and won't go anywhere.

This method works because your stitches physically trap the tail. The resulting work has no visible knot on either side when finished.

Ending Your Thread

Ending securely is just as important as starting. A poor ending will let your stitches unravel from the back.

  1. Take a tiny stitch in the same spot, but don't pull it all the way through — leave a small loop.
  2. Pass the needle through that loop and pull gently until a knot forms close to the fabric surface.
  3. Repeat this once more in the same spot for security.
  4. Trim the thread tail close — about ¼ inch is fine.

The Five Essential Hand Stitches

1. Running Stitch

When to use it: Basting (temporary holding of fabric layers), gathering (bunching fabric into ruffles), simple visible decoration, and hand quilting.

  1. Bring the needle up through the fabric from the wrong side to the right side.
  2. Push the needle back down through the fabric a short distance ahead — this is one stitch. Stitch length for functional work: about ¼ inch. For basting: ½ inch or longer.
  3. Bring the needle back up the same distance ahead as the stitch you just took.
  4. Continue, aiming for even, consistent spacing. The stitches on both sides of the fabric should be the same length.

      ___   ___   ___   ___     thread underneath

2. Backstitch

When to use it: Strong seams, repairs, any situation where you need maximum hand-stitch strength. The backstitch is the strongest hand stitch because each new stitch overlaps the previous one on the underside, creating an interlocked line of thread with no gaps.

  1. Bring the needle up at your starting point.
  2. Push the needle back DOWN one stitch-length behind where you came up — going backward.
  3. Bring the needle up one stitch-length ahead of where you started (so you're now two stitch-lengths past your origin).
  4. Push the needle back down at the end of the last stitch (the forward end of the stitch you already made) — going backward again.
  5. Continue: always go forward one stitch-length underground, then back one stitch-length on the surface. On the surface it looks like a continuous unbroken line of stitches. On the underside it looks like overlapping stitches.

3. Whip Stitch

When to use it: Joining two folded or finished edges together. Often used to attach appliqué, join knitted or crocheted pieces, or close an edge temporarily. Not invisible, but neat and tidy when done evenly.

  1. Hold the two fabric edges together, aligned, with the sides you want to show facing out.
  2. Bring the needle up through both layers from back to front, close to the edge.
  3. Bring the thread over the top of the joined edge diagonally and push the needle through both layers again, slightly ahead of your first stitch.
  4. Continue, keeping each stitch the same length and maintaining a consistent diagonal angle. The thread wraps over the edge with each stitch — this is what gives the whip stitch its name.

4. Slip Stitch (Ladder Stitch)

When to use it: Closing an opening invisibly after stuffing a pillow, turning a bag right-side out, or finishing any project where you need to close a gap with no visible stitches on the outside. When pulled closed, this stitch disappears — the two folded edges appear to be fused together with no stitching showing at all.

  1. Fold both open edges under and press them if possible so you have two clean, folded edges facing each other.
  2. Bring the needle up through one folded edge, starting inside the fold so the knot is hidden inside the fabric.
  3. Directly across, pick up a small amount of the fold on the opposite edge — slide the needle horizontally through about ¼ inch of the fold, parallel to the edge. Don't go all the way through the fabric.
  4. Cross back to the first edge and slide through another ¼ inch of fold, directly across from where you exited the second edge.
  5. Continue alternating sides, pulling the thread gently (not tightly) after every 4–5 stitches. As you pull, the two folded edges draw together and the stitches drop inside the seam, becoming invisible.
  6. End with your secure knot hidden inside the fold.

5. Basting Stitch

When to use it: Temporarily holding fabric layers together before machine sewing, testing a fit before final stitching, holding a zipper in place for fitting, or gathering fabric into ruffles. Basting is always meant to be removed after the final stitching is done.

  1. Use the running stitch technique, but make your stitches very long — ½ inch to 1 inch intervals.
  2. Do not knot the thread securely at the start or end. You want to be able to pull this thread out easily later. Use a simple loop or just a small backstitch to start.
  3. Leave long thread tails at both ends so you have something to grab when it's time to remove the basting.
  4. To remove: snip the thread every few inches and pull the pieces out. Alternatively, pull one long tail — the other end will either pull through or you'll need to snip and pull in sections.

  _____       _____       _____
       _____       _____       _____
  ↑ long gaps ↑   ↑ long gaps ↑
  (½ to 1 inch between stitches)

⚠️ Common Mistake — Pulling the thread too tight. Every hand stitch should lie flat against the fabric surface. If you pull the thread taut after each stitch, the fabric gathers and puckers — and hand-sewn puckers are very difficult to press out. The thread should be snug enough that it doesn't droop or loop, but not so tight that it deforms the fabric. Practice on a scrap piece and aim for a relaxed, even tension.

💡 Tip — Work with thread no longer than 18 inches. This cannot be stated often enough. Long thread tangles, knots, and creates the kind of frustrating mid-project mess that makes people give up for the day. When your thread runs low, end it securely and cut a new length. The small inefficiency of re-threading is far less annoying than untangling a knotted length.
