Finishing

Hems & Finishing

The hem is the finished lower edge of a garment or the finished edge of any sewn item. A good hem is straight, even, and invisible (or deliberately visible as a design detail).

Lesson 9 of 10

What Is a Hem Allowance?

The hem allowance is the extra fabric built into the cutting length of a garment beyond the desired finished length. That extra fabric is what gets folded up to create the hem itself.

Different hem methods require different hem allowances:

Hem Method Typical Allowance
Double-fold machine hem (most garments) 1 inch
Hand-sewn blind hem (dress pants, skirts) 1½ to 2 inches
Narrow machine hem (lightweight fabrics) ¼ inch
Rolled hem (delicate edges) ¼ inch or less

When a pattern includes a hem, the allowance is already included in the pattern piece. The instruction sheet will tell you the hem allowance for each edge. If you are drafting your own measurements (as in the pillow project in Lesson 10), you add the allowance yourself when calculating how much fabric to cut.

Determining Hem Length

For garments, the process of marking the finished hem length is called "marking the hem," and it should always be done while the garment is being worn.

  1. Put on the completed garment and the shoes you plan to wear with it. Heel height changes the apparent length of a hem significantly.
  2. Stand naturally on a hard floor. Have a second person walk around you placing pins at even intervals at the desired finished length, keeping the marker parallel to the floor (not parallel to the hem edge, which may not be level).
  3. Alternatively, use a hem marker tool — a small device that blows chalk at a set height — to mark the line yourself.
  4. Remove the garment and measure up from the marked line by the hem allowance amount. Trim the fabric at this new line.
  5. Fold, press, and stitch the hem using your chosen method.

For home décor items like curtains and table linens, measure directly from the finished item or follow the project pattern. Press and check the fold before stitching — once it's sewn, correcting an uneven hem is tedious.

Method 1: Double-Fold Machine Hem (Most Common)

The double-fold machine hem is the workhorse of sewing. It encases the raw edge inside two folds, leaving no raw fabric exposed, and is secured with a straight machine stitch visible on the right side of the garment. It's used on jeans, casual skirts, curtains, napkins, bags — almost everything.

Step by step

  1. With the garment wrong side up, fold the raw edge up by ¼ inch toward the wrong side. Press this fold crisp all the way around.
  2. Fold up again by the desired finished hem depth (typically ¾ inch for a 1-inch hem allowance, giving ¼ inch for the first fold and ¾ inch for the second). Press this second fold thoroughly.
  3. Pin at regular intervals, or use hem clips to hold the fold in place while sewing.
  4. Stitch close to the inner folded edge — about 1/8 inch from the fold — all the way around. Keep your speed steady and the fold even under the presser foot.
  5. Optional: add a second row of topstitching parallel to the hem edge for a more finished, decorative look.

The key to a flat, even double-fold hem is pressing. Press each fold before stitching. The iron does the work of keeping the fold in place so the machine can stitch it accurately.

Method 2: Hand Blind Hem (Slip Stitch)

A well-executed hand blind hem is invisible from the right side of the garment — the gold standard for dress pants, formal skirts, and lined curtains. The stitches are hidden between the hem allowance and the garment, catching only one or two threads of the outer fabric with each stitch.

Step by step

  1. Finish the raw edge of the hem allowance first (zigzag stitch or serger).
  2. Fold the hem up by the allowance amount and press well. Pin in place.
  3. Thread a hand-sewing needle with thread that matches the fabric. Use a single thread, no more than 18 inches long.
  4. With the garment right side facing you, fold the hem allowance back on itself by about ¼ inch so the finished edge is visible and the fold-back creates a small roll of hem allowance between the garment and the edge.
  5. Working right to left (or left to right — whichever feels natural), take a tiny stitch in the garment fabric, catching only one or two threads from the surface. Then take a stitch in the folded hem allowance, traveling about ¼ inch along the allowance. Alternate between garment and hem with each stitch.
  6. Do not pull tight. The stitch should be just snug enough to hold — pulling creates puckers visible from the right side.

Viewed from the right side, the only visible sign of a slip-stitched blind hem should be a faint dimple where each stitch caught the outer fabric — and even that disappears with pressing and wear.

Method 3: Machine Blind Hem

The machine blind hem stitch mimics the hand slip stitch at machine speed. It requires a special blind hem presser foot and the blind hem stitch on your machine.

Step by step

  1. Fold and press the hem allowance to the wrong side. Finish the raw edge if you haven't already.
  2. With the garment right side facing you, fold the hem allowance back so the folded edge extends about ¼ inch beyond the garment fold — creating a step between the hem allowance and the garment.
  3. Attach the blind hem presser foot. Adjust the guide so it rides along the fold of the garment fabric.
  4. Select the blind hem stitch on your machine. This stitch makes several straight stitches in the hem allowance followed by one wide V-stitch that bites sideways into the fold of the garment.
  5. Sew, keeping the guide firmly against the fold. The straight stitches stay in the hem allowance; the occasional V-stitch just barely bites into the garment fold.
  6. Open out and press. The V-stitches should be nearly invisible — just a small dimple in the fabric.

Always test on a scrap first. Adjust the stitch width so the V-stitch catches only one or two threads. If the bite is too wide, the stitch shows prominently on the right side.

Narrow Rolled Hem

A narrow rolled hem creates a very fine, delicate finished edge with minimal bulk. It's the hem used on silk scarves, ruffles, chiffon edges, and the border of cloth napkins.

How to sew it

  1. Trim the hem allowance to ¼ inch.
  2. Fold under ⅛ inch of the raw edge toward the wrong side and press lightly.
  3. Fold under another ⅛ inch and press.
  4. Stitch close to the second fold — as close as ⅛ inch from the folded edge. A shorter stitch length (1.5 mm) works best for the control needed on such a narrow fold.

A rolled hem foot attachment makes this process much easier by automatically rolling the edge as you feed the fabric. If your machine came with a narrow hem foot, this is the time to use it.

The result is a hem that adds almost no bulk and barely changes the drape of lightweight fabric — a stark contrast to the double-fold hem, which adds a noticeable layer on anything sheer or lightweight.

⚠️ Common Mistake — Making the hem uneven. The most common cause is inconsistent fold depth: the fold is ¾ inch in one spot and ½ inch in another, producing a wavy, irregular hem line. Measure and press the hem fold before stitching using a hem gauge — a small metal ruler with a sliding guide that sets to a fixed measurement. Slide the guide to your desired fold depth, fold fabric against it, and press. Move along the hem a few inches at a time, always using the gauge as your guide.

💡 Tip — Press each fold crisp before stitching. A pressed hem stays exactly where you put it while feeding through the machine. An unpressed hem shifts, drifts, and creates ripples or uneven depth along the stitch line. Two minutes at the ironing board before you sit at the machine saves the frustration of ripping out and redoing a wavy hem.
