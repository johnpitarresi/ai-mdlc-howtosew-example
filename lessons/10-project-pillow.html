First Project

Project: Pillow Cover

You've learned the skills. Now let's use them. A pillow cover is the perfect first project: it uses a straight seam, basic seam finishing, and pressing — with a satisfying result you can actually use.

Lesson 10 of 10

What You'll Make

An envelope-back pillow cover for a 16×16 inch pillow insert. The back uses two overlapping fabric panels — the "envelope" construction — that let you insert and remove the pillow without any zipper or button closure. The overlap holds naturally under tension once the pillow is inside.

This construction method is used on high-end throw pillows, hotel bedding, and professional home décor. It looks intentional and refined, and it's actually simpler to sew than any closure.

Materials

  • ½ yard of woven cotton fabric (pre-washed — see Step 1)
  • Matching all-purpose thread
  • 16×16 inch pillow insert
  • Fabric scissors (not paper scissors)
  • Pins or Wonder Clips
  • Iron and ironing board
  • Ruler or measuring tape
  • Fabric marker or tailor's chalk
  • Optional: pinking shears or access to zigzag stitch for seam finishing

Choose a medium-weight woven cotton — quilting cotton, home décor cotton, or cotton canvas all work well. Avoid very lightweight fabrics for your first project; they're harder to handle. Avoid stretchy knits — this construction requires a stable woven fabric.

Cut List

Piece Dimensions Quantity
Front panel 17 × 17 inches 1
Back panel 17 × 12 inches 2

The 1 inch of extra on each side of the 17×17 front panel accounts for a ½ inch seam allowance on all four edges. The two 17×12 back panels, when assembled with a hemmed center edge and layered over each other, create a back panel that totals more than 17 inches across — the overlap is intentional and creates the envelope closure. Cutting accurately makes every step that follows easier and more precise.

Project Steps

Step 1

Pre-wash and press fabric

Lesson 2: Fabric Fundamentals

Wash and dry your fabric using the same method you'll use to launder the finished pillow cover. Then press it smooth with your iron before cutting — wrinkled fabric cannot be measured or cut accurately.

Do this step now, before you cut a single piece. Pre-washing removes the sizing (stiffening agents applied during manufacturing), shrinks the fabric to its finished dimensions, and ensures the finished pillow cover won't shrink on its first wash.

⚠️ Common Mistake — Skipping pre-washing. Your pillow cover will shrink on the first wash if you sew with unwashed fabric. A 17×17 inch cover made from unwashed cotton can shrink to 16×16 inches or smaller — exactly the size of the insert — leaving no ease and a puckered, overstuffed result.

Step 2

Cut your pieces

Lesson 8: Reading Patterns / Measuring

Using your ruler and fabric marker, measure and mark your three pieces directly on the fabric:

  • One front panel: 17 × 17 inches
  • Two back panels: 17 × 12 inches each

Align your measurements with the grain of the fabric — the lengthwise grain should run parallel to the selvage. A pillow cut off-grain will not lie flat and may twist slightly over time.

Double-check every measurement before you cut. Measure twice, cut once is not a cliché — it's the most practical advice in sewing. Fabric is easy to cut smaller; it cannot be cut larger.

Step 3

Finish the center edges of the back panels

Lesson 6: Seam Finishing

The two back panels will overlap in the center of the pillow back. The edges where they overlap — the 17-inch long edge on each back panel — will be visible from inside the pillow when it's turned right side out. These edges need a clean hem so they look tidy and don't fray.

On each back panel:

  1. Identify the 17-inch edge that will sit at the center (not the outer edges that will be in the seam).
  2. Fold this edge ¼ inch toward the wrong side. Press the fold crisp.
  3. Fold again another ¼ inch toward the wrong side. Press again.
  4. Stitch close to the inner fold to secure the double-fold hem.

Repeat on both back panels. These are the only edges of the project that will be visible in the finished interior of the cover.

Step 4

Assemble the envelope back

Lesson 5: Your First Seam

This step assembles all three pieces into a layered sandwich ready for sewing. The pieces are layered with right sides together (in contact) and wrong sides facing outward.

  1. Lay the front panel wrong side up on your work surface. All four raw edges should be visible around the perimeter.
  2. Lay one back panel right side up on top of the front panel, aligning the raw edges on three sides: left edge, top edge, right edge. The hemmed center edge sits in the middle of the front panel, not at the outer edge.
  3. Lay the second back panel right side up on top, also aligning its raw edges with the left, top, and right edges of the front panel. Its hemmed center edge overlaps the first back panel's hemmed center edge by several inches. This is the envelope overlap.
  4. Check that all raw outer edges are neatly aligned on all four sides. The two back panels should together cover the full 17-inch width of the front panel, with their hemmed edges overlapping generously in the center.
  5. Pin through all layers at regular intervals around all four outer edges.
Step 5

Sew all four sides

Lesson 5: Your First Seam

Sew around all four sides of the pinned assembly with a ½ inch seam allowance. Keep the seam allowance consistent all the way around — inconsistent seam allowances make the corners uneven.

  1. Begin on one side (not a corner). Backstitch three to four stitches at the start to lock the thread.
  2. Sew to the first corner, stopping with the needle down exactly ½ inch from the edge of the corner.
  3. With the needle down, raise the presser foot. Pivot the fabric 90 degrees. Lower the presser foot. Continue sewing.
  4. Repeat at each corner. Backstitch at the end of the last seam to secure.

Sewing with the needle down at pivot points is critical — it holds the fabric in place while you rotate it, preventing the corner from shifting and producing clean, square 90-degree corners.

Step 6

Finish the seam allowances

Lesson 6: Seam Finishing

Before trimming corners, run a zigzag stitch along all four seam allowances (or trim with pinking shears) to prevent fraying. Then address the corners:

  1. Trim the corners diagonally. At each corner, cut across the seam allowance at a 45-degree angle, stopping 1/8 inch from the corner stitching. Do not cut through the stitches. This removes the bulk of the seam allowance fabric that would otherwise bunch inside the corner when the cover is turned right side out.
  2. If desired, trim slightly more from the two seam allowances adjacent to each corner to further reduce bulk.

Taking the time to trim corners properly is the difference between pillow corners that look sharp and square versus corners that look lumpy and rounded from the inside out.

Step 7

Press the seams

Lesson 7: Pressing

Before turning the cover right side out, press the seam allowances from the wrong side. This sets the stitches into the fabric and makes the seams lie as flat as possible once the cover is turned.

  1. Open the seam allowances as best you can inside the sewn square (it's tight — use the tip of the iron and work carefully).
  2. Pay special attention to the corners: press each corner with the point of the iron to push the seam allowances flat toward the sides.
  3. Use a point turner (a blunt-tipped tool) or the eraser end of a pencil to help push the corners fully flat from the inside.
Step 8

Turn right side out and press

Reach your hand through the envelope opening in the back of the cover and pull the fabric right side out. Take your time — pulling too forcefully can stress the corner seams.

  1. Once turned, use a point turner, a blunt pencil, or a chopstick to gently push out each corner from the inside. Work slowly and push from the inside outward. Do not use scissors — the sharp point will poke through the seam.
  2. Press the entire cover flat on the ironing board, paying special attention to the seam lines and all four corners. The seam lines should sit right at the edge of the cover, not rolled to the back or front.
  3. Press the corners until they are sharply square.
Step 9

Insert the pillow and enjoy

Fold the pillow insert in half (compress it), push it through the envelope opening in the back of the cover, and unfold it inside. Work the corners of the insert into the corners of the cover.

The envelope overlap should hold the pillow securely. If it gaps open significantly, the overlap in Step 4 may not have been generous enough — this is easy to account for on the next cover by making the back panels slightly larger (13 or 14 inches instead of 12).

Your pillow cover is complete.
