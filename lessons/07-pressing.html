Pressing

Pressing is not ironing. It's a specific technique that sets your seams, shapes your fabric, and is the single biggest difference between a home-sewn item that looks professional and one that looks homemade.

Lesson 7 of 10

Pressing vs. Ironing

Most people use an iron two ways without distinguishing between them — but in sewing, the difference is critical.

Ironing
Sliding the iron across fabric in long, gliding strokes to remove wrinkles. This is what you do to a shirt before wearing it. The movement is horizontal and continuous.
Pressing
Lifting the iron and placing it firmly down onto a specific area of the fabric, holding it briefly, then lifting it again and moving to the next position. The movement is vertical — up and down, not side to side.

When you slide an iron over a freshly sewn seam, the downward pressure combined with horizontal movement drags the fabric sideways. On the bias or in any area that isn't completely stable, this stretches and distorts the fabric permanently. A seam that was perfectly straight and the correct length can become rippled, stretched, or warped from a single pass of a sliding iron.

The rule is simple: always press, never iron — lift, position, press down firmly, hold two to three seconds, lift, move, repeat.

Why Pressing Matters So Much

Ask any experienced seamstress what separates beginner work from professional work, and pressing is almost always at the top of the list. It matters at every stage of a project:

  • Before cutting: Press your fabric smooth so pattern pieces lie flat and can be cut accurately.
  • After each seam: Press the seam flat as sewn first (this sets the stitches into the fabric). Then press it open or to one side depending on the construction method.
  • Before crossing a seam: Any time a second seam will cross over the first, the first seam must be pressed. Sewing over an unpressed, lumpy seam shifts your stitching line and creates inaccuracy that compounds through every subsequent step.
  • During shaping: Darts, curves, and shaped edges are all set with pressing. The iron is a shaping tool, not just a flattening tool.

A pressed seam lies completely flat, making every subsequent step easier and more accurate. An unpressed seam creates a ridge under the fabric — and every piece you lay on top of it, pin to it, or sew across it is affected by that ridge.

Pressing Seams Open

Pressing a seam open means spreading the two seam allowances apart, one to each side, so the seam itself lies in the center and forms the flattest possible surface.

How to do it

  1. Lay the sewn piece over your ironing board, wrong side up, with the seam line running lengthwise along the board.
  2. Use the tip of your finger (carefully — steam burns) or a seam roller to spread the two seam allowances apart, one toward each side.
  3. Position the iron over the seam, press firmly, hold two to three seconds, lift, and move to the next section. Work along the entire seam length.
  4. Flip to the right side and give it a final press from the top to ensure the seam line is crisp and flat.

When to use it

  • Any seam that will be topstitched (the stitching line needs to be perfectly flat on both sides)
  • Jeans-style construction and structured garments where maximum flatness is needed
  • Quilting seams when you want the seam line to lie as flat as possible
  • Any seam at a corner or intersection where bulk is a concern

Pressing Seams to One Side

Instead of spreading the allowances apart, pressing to one side means folding both seam allowances together toward one direction — usually toward the darker fabric or toward the side specified in your pattern instructions.

How to do it

  1. With the piece wrong side up, use your finger to fold both seam allowances to the same side.
  2. Press firmly along the seam, working from one end to the other.
  3. A small ridge will form at the seam line — this is intentional and creates a stronger construction.

When to use it

  • Quilting blocks, where the seam-to-one-side method nests seams neatly at intersections
  • Children's garments and sportswear, where durability is more important than minimal bulk
  • Any seam that will be under repeated stress — shoulder seams, crotch seams, bag straps

Pressing Curved Seams

Curved seams present a challenge: the seam allowance is trying to lie flat while the fabric it's attached to curves in three dimensions. Before pressing, you need to prepare the seam allowance so it can open up and conform to the curve.

Clipping and notching

  • Concave curves (curves that curve inward, like a neckline): Clip small straight cuts into the seam allowance, perpendicular to the seam line, stopping 1/8 inch from the stitching. The clips allow the seam allowance to spread open as the fabric curves.
  • Convex curves (curves that curve outward, like a rounded hem): Cut small V-shaped notches out of the seam allowance. Removing fabric prevents the allowance from bunching when it bends inward.

Using a tailor's ham

A tailor's ham is a firmly stuffed, ham-shaped pressing cushion. Placing a curved seam over the ham's curve before pressing allows the iron to press the seam while it's already in the shape it will hold. Without a ham, you can use a tightly rolled, pinned towel as a substitute.

Press from the wrong side first to set the seam, then use a pressing cloth on the right side for a final smooth press.

Tools for Pressing

  • Iron: A steam iron is strongly preferred. Steam helps set the fabric fibers quickly. A dry iron works but requires more effort and sometimes a damp pressing cloth.
  • Ironing board: A well-padded, stable board. The narrow end is useful for pressing sleeves and other tubular pieces.
  • Pressing cloth: A thin piece of smooth cotton fabric (muslin or a clean cotton handkerchief) placed between the iron and the project fabric. Protects delicate fabrics from iron shine and prevents scorching.
  • Seam roller: A small wooden or rubber roller used to press seams open in areas too small for the iron tip. Useful on curved seams and tight corners.
  • Tailor's ham: Stuffed pressing cushion for curves and darts. Optional for beginners, but very useful as projects become more complex.
  • Sleeve board: A small, narrow ironing board that fits inside a sleeve or other narrow tube. Lets you press without creating a crease on the opposite side.

Iron Heat Settings by Fabric

Fabric Heat Setting Notes
Cotton High heat, steam Very heat-tolerant; presses beautifully and holds a crease well
Linen High heat, steam Press while still slightly damp for best results; resistant to wrinkles when dry
Wool Medium heat, steam Always use a pressing cloth to prevent shine; steam helps relax the fibers
Polyester Low heat, no steam Melts at high heat — always test on a scrap before pressing a seam
Silk Low heat, no steam Extremely heat-sensitive; always use a pressing cloth; water spots easily
Blends Medium heat Default to the most heat-sensitive fiber in the blend; always test on a scrap

When working with any unfamiliar fabric, always press a scrap piece first. This takes thirty seconds and can save you from scorching or melting the actual project fabric.

⚠️ Common Mistake — Pressing is not optional. Skipping it is the single most common reason beginner sewing looks "homemade." Even a simple pillow cover looks dramatically better when every seam is pressed before the next step. Make it a rule without exceptions: sew a seam, press a seam. The two or three minutes spent at the ironing board after each seam saves much more time correcting problems later.

💡 Tip — Press each seam before sewing across it. If you sew over an unpressed seam, you're sewing over a lump — which shifts your stitch line off course and creates inaccuracy that compounds through the whole project. The seam that crosses it won't lie flat. The piece assembled from those seams won't match its neighboring piece. One unpressed seam can cause problems several steps downstream.
