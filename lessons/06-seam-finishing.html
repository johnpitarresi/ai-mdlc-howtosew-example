Seam Finishing

A raw seam allowance will fray over time. Seam finishing protects your seam allowances from unraveling — and it's the difference between a project that lasts and one that falls apart.

Lesson 6 of 10

Why Finish Seams?

Woven fabric is made of interlocking threads. When you cut it, the threads at the cut edge have nothing holding them in place — and with handling, washing, and wear, they begin to slide out. This is called fraying.

Fraying seam allowances don't stay contained to the edge. Over time, the loose threads migrate toward the seam itself, pulling the stitches apart and weakening the entire construction. A seam that looked perfectly fine after sewing can fail after just a few washes if the allowances were left raw.

All finished garments — whether made in a factory or a home studio — have some form of seam finishing on the inside. The method varies by the garment type, the fabric, and the tools available, but the purpose is always the same: stop the fray before it reaches the seam.

Home décor items like pillows are a partial exception. Because they're rarely washed aggressively, raw seams often survive — but finishing them is still good practice, and it's what separates a careful project from a hasty one.

Method 1: Pinking Shears

Pinking shears are specialty scissors with a zigzag blade. Instead of a straight cut, they leave a sawtooth edge on the fabric. That zigzag interrupts the parallel threads at the edge, making them less likely to slide out in a long, continuous fray.

How to use them

  1. Sew your seam as normal and press it open or to one side.
  2. Trim along each raw edge of the seam allowance with the pinking shears, cutting off just a small sliver — enough to create the zigzag edge without removing too much allowance.
  3. The result is a neat, triangular-toothed edge that resists fraying significantly better than a straight cut.

Best for

  • Tightly woven fabrics such as quilting cotton, poplin, and canvas
  • Quick projects where speed matters more than perfection

Limitations

  • Not ideal for loosely woven fabrics like linen or burlap — the long floats still slide out between the zigzag teeth
  • Does not work on knit fabrics, which stretch and distort under the blade
  • Not a permanent solution on anything that will be washed repeatedly and heavily

Method 2: Zigzag Stitch

The zigzag stitch is the most widely used seam-finishing method for home sewers. Almost every standard sewing machine has a zigzag setting, and it works reliably on nearly all woven fabrics.

The zigzag wraps each thread at the raw edge, locking it in place. Unlike pinking, it doesn't remove any fabric — it encases the edge in stitching.

Settings

  • Stitch width: 3 mm (medium — wide enough to wrap the edge but not so wide it puckers)
  • Stitch length: 2 mm (medium — short enough to hold, long enough not to stiffen)

Step by step

  1. Sew your seam with a straight stitch and press it open or to one side.
  2. Set your machine to the zigzag stitch and dial in the width and length above.
  3. Align the raw edge of one seam allowance under the presser foot so the right swing of the needle just clears the fabric edge (or falls off it by 1–2 mm).
  4. Sew along the raw edge. The stitches should straddle the edge — one side on the fabric, one side off.
  5. Repeat on the second seam allowance.

Finish each seam allowance separately. Do not zigzag through both allowances together while pressing them to one side — this creates a stiff, bulky ridge.

Method 3: Clean Finish (Folded Edge)

A clean finish encloses the raw edge inside a fold of the seam allowance itself. There is no raw thread visible at all — just a neatly folded, stitched edge. This is the most labor-intensive of the three basic methods, but it produces the most polished interior.

Step by step

  1. Sew your seam and press it open.
  2. On one seam allowance, fold 1/4 inch of the raw edge toward the wrong side. Press that fold crisp with the iron.
  3. Stitch close to the folded edge — about 1/8 inch from the fold — to lock it in place.
  4. Repeat on the second seam allowance.

Best for

  • Lightweight fabrics where the fold doesn't add too much bulk (cotton voile, lawn, batiste, light quilting cotton)
  • Unlined jackets and garments where the interior will be visible and should look clean
  • Any project where the inside is as important as the outside

Limitations

  • Adds a double layer to the seam allowance, which can create bulk in heavier fabrics
  • Takes significantly more time than pinking or zigzag
  • Difficult to execute on curved seams without clipping first

Which Method to Use?

Method Speed Best Fabrics Avoid On
Pinking shears Very fast Tightly woven cottons, canvas, poplin Knits, loose weaves, anything washed heavily
Zigzag stitch Fast All woven fabrics, most knits Very thick fabrics (use a serger instead)
Clean finish Slow Lightweight wovens, unlined garments Heavy fabrics, tight curves

When in doubt, use the zigzag stitch. It's the default finishing method for a reason — it's fast, effective, and works on almost everything a standard home sewer encounters.

⚠️ Common Mistake — Skipping seam finishing entirely, especially on cotton. After the first wash, the seam allowances fray and the threads work into the seam itself, weakening the entire construction. What looked fine off the machine can fall apart in the laundry after just a few cycles.

💡 Tip — Always press your seam before finishing the edges. Pressing first makes it much easier to guide the seam allowance cleanly through the zigzag or folded-edge finish. An unpressed seam allowance wanders under the foot and creates uneven, sloppy stitching along the edge.
